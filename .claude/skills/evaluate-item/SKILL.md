# Evaluate Item

Identify what the item is, assess its condition, and surface the value drivers that matter for pricing and listing. This skill covers all supported collectible categories.

---

## Trading Cards (MTG, Pokemon, Sports)

### Key details to gather
- **Game/sport** — MTG, Pokemon, sports (baseball, basketball, football, hockey)
- **Card name and set** — exact card name, set name, set code, collector number
- **Year/release** — when the set was released
- **Variant** — regular, foil/holo, full art, extended art, showcase, borderless, reverse holo, refractor, prizm
- **Edition** — 1st edition (Pokemon/Yugioh), unlimited, shadowless (Pokemon Base Set)
- **Graded or raw** — if graded: company (PSA/BGS/CGC), grade, cert number
- **Language** — English, Japanese, other (Japanese Pokemon cards have separate market)

### Raw card condition scale
Use these standard conditions for ungraded cards:

| Condition | Abbrev | Description |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Near Mint | NM | Minimal wear. Crisp corners, clean edges, no scratches or scuffs. Slight whitening on one edge acceptable |
| Lightly Played | LP | Minor edge wear, light scuffs or scratches. No structural damage. Still looks good in a binder |
| Moderately Played | MP | Noticeable edge wear, surface scratches, minor creasing. Playable but visibly worn |
| Heavily Played | HP | Major wear throughout — creasing, whitening, border wear. Still intact but rough |
| Damaged | DMG | Torn, water-damaged, stained, heavy creasing, missing ink. May not be tournament-legal |

### Professional grading scales

**PSA (1–10, no half points)**
- PSA 10 (Gem Mint): Perfect or near-perfect. Highest tier, biggest premium
- PSA 9 (Mint): One minor flaw. Typically 30–50% of PSA 10 value
- PSA 8 (NM-MT): A few minor flaws visible on close inspection
- PSA 7 and below: Diminishing returns for modern cards; still valuable for vintage

**BGS (1–10, with subgrades)**
- Subgrades: Centering, Corners, Edges, Surface — each scored separately
- BGS 9.5 (Gem Mint): Premium grade. Competitive with PSA 10 in some markets
- BGS 10 (Pristine): Extremely rare. All four subgrades must be 10
- Black Label 10: All subgrades are 10 — displayed on a black label. Ultra-premium

**CGC (1–10, with half-point increments)**
- CGC 10 (Pristine): Equivalent to a perfect card
- CGC 9.5 (Gem Mint): Comparable to BGS 9.5/PSA 10
- Half-point increments allow more nuance than PSA
- Growing market share, especially for Pokemon cards under $500

### Grading value multipliers (rough guides)
- PSA 10 vs raw NM: typically 2–5x for modern, 5–20x for vintage/key cards
- PSA 10 vs PSA 9: PSA 10 is often 2–4x the PSA 9 price
- BGS 9.5 ≈ PSA 10 in value for most modern cards
- BGS Black Label 10 > PSA 10, sometimes significantly

### Game-specific notes

**MTG:**
- Centering is the #1 grade-limiter for MTG cards
- Foil curling (pringles) is common and doesn't affect grade if card is otherwise mint
- Reserve List cards (e.g., dual lands, Power Nine) have guaranteed scarcity — always note if a card is on the RL
- Commander/EDH staples hold value differently than Standard-legal cards

**Pokemon:**
- Shadowless Base Set cards are significantly more valuable than Unlimited
- 1st Edition stamp adds major premium (especially Base Set, Jungle, Fossil)
- Japanese cards have a separate and active market — don't assume lower value
- Modern chase cards: alternate art, special art rare (SAR), illustration rare (IR)

**Sports:**
- Rookie cards (RC) are the primary value driver — always identify if it's a rookie
- Parallels matter enormously: base < silver prizm < gold < black < 1/1
- Serial numbering (/25, /10, /5, /1) — lower print run = higher value
- Auto/patch/relic cards: verify on-card auto vs sticker auto (on-card commands premium)

## Sealed Card Products

### Key details to gather
- **Game** — MTG, Pokemon, sports brand (Topps, Panini, Upper Deck)
- **Product type** — booster box, ETB, blister pack, bundle, fat pack, collector box
- **Set name** — which set/expansion
- **Edition** — 1st edition, unlimited, or print wave if applicable
- **Seal integrity** — factory shrink wrap present? Wizards/Pokemon Company seal? Any tears or resealing evidence?
- **Language** — English, Japanese, other

### What drives sealed product value
- **Out of print** — once a set stops being printed, sealed product appreciates
- **Set popularity** — iconic sets (Pokemon Base Set, MTG Alpha/Beta, Chronicles) hold premium
- **Chase cards inside** — sets with high-value chase cards drive box prices up
- **Edition/print run** — 1st edition or early print runs are significantly rarer
- **Product type** — booster boxes generally hold best; ETBs and special products vary

### Seal integrity assessment
- **Factory sealed**: Original shrink wrap with manufacturer's seal/logo intact
- **Distributor sealed**: Overwrap from a distributor — not the same as factory. Note this
- **Re-sealed risk indicators**: Loose or wrinkled shrink wrap, missing manufacturer logo on seal, seam irregularities
- Always disclose seal condition honestly — it's one of the first things buyers check

### Pokemon sealed specifics
- Base Set 1st Edition booster box: six-figure item — authentication critical
- Modern ETBs: look for the Pokemon Company International seal
- Booster box count: standard is 36 packs (English) or 30 packs (Japanese)
- "God packs" (all hits) exist in some Japanese sets — note if relevant

### MTG sealed specifics
- Draft booster box: 36 packs (standard), Play booster box: 36 packs
- Collector booster box: 12 packs (premium product, higher per-pack price)
- Set booster box: 30 packs (discontinued format but still traded)
- Vintage sealed (pre-Modern): authentication is critical — look for Wizards seal

## Funko Pops

### Key details to gather
- **Character and line** — which character, which franchise/license
- **Pop number** — the number on the box (e.g., #01)
- **Variant type** — common, chase, exclusive, flocked, glow-in-the-dark, metallic, diamond
- **Exclusive sticker** — which store/convention (Hot Topic, Target, SDCC, NYCC, Funko Shop, etc.)
- **Vaulted status** — has Funko retired this mold? (check PPG/HobbyDB)
- **Box condition** — this is critical for Funko value

### Box condition grading

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Mint | No visible flaws. Corners sharp, window clear, no creases or dents. Factory-fresh appearance |
| Near Mint | 1–2 extremely minor flaws visible only on close inspection. A tiny scuff or barely perceptible crease |
| Very Good | Visible but minor shelf wear — small creases, light corner dings, minor window scratches |
| Good | Moderate wear — noticeable creases, corner damage, small dents. Still displayable |
| Fair | Significant damage — major creases, crushed corners, window damage. Figure may be fine but box is rough |
| Poor/OOB | Box too damaged to matter, or out-of-box (no box). Value drops 40–60% vs mint box |

### Value drivers
- **Vaulted + popular character** = strongest combo. Vaulted means Funko stopped producing it
- **Chase variants**: 1-in-6 ratio in standard cases. Visually distinct from the common (different pose, color, glow, etc.)
- **Convention exclusives**: SDCC, NYCC, ECCC — shared exclusives (store stickers) are less than true con stickers
- **Store exclusives**: Hot Topic, Target, Walmart, Amazon, BoxLunch, Funko Shop — sticker must be present
- **Numbered/limited pieces**: Some are limited to specific piece counts (e.g., LE 3000)
- **Early production runs**: First-wave Pops (#01–#30 in a line) often have long-term value
- **Sticker matters**: Correct exclusive sticker significantly impacts value. Wrong sticker or missing sticker = lower price

### Red flags
- Missing or replaced sticker
- Box swap (common Pop in a rare box)
- Paint defects beyond normal Funko QC variation
- Crushed or water-damaged boxes

## Disney Pins

### Key details to gather
- **Pin name/design** — what's depicted, which character(s)
- **Series or collection** — which release series, event, or park
- **Edition type** — Limited Edition (LE), Open Edition (OE), Hidden Mickey, mystery, rack
- **LE count** — if limited edition, what's the edition size?
- **Back stamp** — what text/markings appear on the back
- **Pin posts** — how many, what type (standard, rubber mickey-head clutch)
- **Year of release** — if known

### Edition types and relative value

| Type | Description | Value tier |
|------|-------------|------------|
| LE (numbered, < 250) | Individually numbered, very small run | Highest |
| LE (numbered, 250–500) | Numbered, small run | Very high |
| LE (not numbered, 500–2000) | Limited but not individually tracked | High |
| LE (2000–5000) | Larger limited run | Moderate–High |
| Hidden Mickey | Cast member traded, waffle back | Moderate (set-dependent) |
| Mystery/blind box | Random from a set | Low–Moderate |
| Rack/Open Edition | Mass-produced park merchandise | Low |

### Authentication quick checks
- **Weight**: Authentic OE pins weigh 4–6 grams. Counterfeits often feel lighter (2–3g) or heavier
- **Back stamp**: Should read "© Disney" (or "© Disney/Pixar" etc.) crisp and clear. Smudged or blurry text = suspect
- **Pin posts**: Authentic pins use smooth, consistent pin posts. Rough or uneven posts suggest counterfeit
- **Enamel quality**: Colors should be vibrant and contained within metal borders. Bleeding or dull colors = suspect
- **Mickey-head clutch backs**: Original Disney clutch backs have clean Mickey head shape

### Official vs Fantasy vs Counterfeit
- **Official**: Manufactured by Disney or licensed partner. Has proper back stamp, appears in PinPics database
- **Fantasy**: Fan-made pins of Disney characters in designs Disney never produced. Not counterfeit (original design) but also not official. Cannot be traded at Disney parks. Often higher quality than counterfeits
- **Counterfeit/scrapper**: Direct copies of official pins. Lower quality — look for rough edges, paint bleeding, light weight. Common in bulk lots from overseas

### Value drivers
- Edition size (smaller = more valuable)
- Character popularity (villains, Stitch, classics tend to hold value)
- Event pins (D23, pin trading events) have built-in scarcity
- Complete sets are worth more than individual pins from a set
- Slider, spinner, hinged, or other mechanical elements add appeal

## General Collectibles (Fallback)

For items outside the specific categories above, gather:

- **What is it?** — exact item, brand, model, year, version
- **Condition** — use a simple scale: Mint / Excellent / Good / Fair / Poor
- **Completeness** — all original parts, accessories, manuals, packaging?
- **Packaging** — original box? Is the box in good condition? Was it opened?
- **Provenance** — any history of ownership, receipts, certificates of authenticity?
- **Markings** — serial numbers, manufacturer stamps, edition numbers, country of origin

### General condition assessment
- **Mint/New**: Unused, undamaged, complete. May or may not be in original packaging
- **Excellent**: Minimal signs of use or display. No damage, all parts present
- **Good**: Shows normal use/display wear. Minor scuffs, scratches. Complete
- **Fair**: Noticeable wear, may be missing minor accessories. Still functional/displayable
- **Poor**: Heavy wear, damage, or incomplete. Value is primarily in the item's rarity, not condition
